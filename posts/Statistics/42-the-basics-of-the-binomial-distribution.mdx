## 3. What is binomial distribution?

The binomial distribution is the specific probability distribution used throughout probability theory and statistics allowing for either success or failure as the possible results of experimentation. For example, once we flip a coin, the only two probable results: are heads or tails, however, if, we take a test, there are only two solutions: pass or fail. A binomial probability distribution would be another name for this distribution.

## 4. What is a binomial probability distribution?

In a binomial probability distribution, the total number of "Successes" in a group of n tests is described either by achievements (probability p) or failure (probability q = 1- p), relying on the results' boolean value. A Bernoulli trial or test is another name for only one success or failure test, whereas a Bernoulli process is another name for a set of trials. For n = 1, or one experiment, the binomial distribution is a Bernoulli distribution. The highly regarded binomial test of statistical significance is established on the binomial distribution.


**Note:**



* There needs to be a predefined number of trials conducted.
* Each trial needs to be independent.
* Between one trial to another, the likelihood of achieving success is exactly the same.


## 5. What is negative binomial distribution?

The frequency of achievements in a set of individually distributed Bernoulli trials prior to the actual specific number of failures in attempts that happens in probability theory and statistics. The negative binomial distribution is the term applied to it. Therefore, "r" signifies the number of failures in attempts. Suppose the dice are rolled and labeled any 1s as failures in attempts and any non-1s as achievements. The probability range for the number of non-1s that were received would take the negative binomial distribution provided that we have thrown the dice frequently until 1 came the third time, i.e., r = 3 failures.


## 6. Uses for the binomial distribution.

Binomial distribution, of which we were previously aware, provides the likelihood of a range of outcomes. The approach is applied in real-world for:



* For computing the amount of natural and consumed materials required to make a commodity.
* Exploring the public's thoughts, both favorable and unfavorable, concerning a specific customer experience or site.
* We can evaluate the number of times people are watching a specific channel via the YES/NO question.
* To establish the percentage of male and female staff in a company.
* A politician's complete vote count is tallied in an election using a probability of 0 or 1.
