## 6. Example of The Null Hypothesis

The average assessment result at one school, as per the headmaster, equals seven out of ten. The population means for 7.0 would be the null hypothesis. For testing the null hypothesis, we collected the scores of, around, 30 students for our sample from the school's combined population of, around, 300, and calculated the sample mean.

Consequently, in an approach to reject the null hypothesis, we might differentiate the calculated sample mean from the hypothesized population mean of 7.0. The sample data is not sufficient to show the null hypothesis, which assumes that the population mean is 7.0. Another solution is to reject it.

In the example, the null hypothesis is that the school's students on average obtained a score of seven out of ten in the assessment.

The null hypothesis is assumed to be true in the interest of the argument to decide whether to reject it. Within this assumption, the estimated statistic's expected range of achievable values for the average test score between 30 students is determined. For example, the range of plausible averages might range from 6.2 to 7.8 if the population mean is 7.0. The null hypothesis is hence rejected when the sample average occurs beyond this range. However, as the difference occurs within the range that is defined by coincidence simply, then it is said to be "explainable by chance alone."
